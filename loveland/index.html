Recognizing the Signs: Do You Need Termite Control?

Termite infestations can be subtle initially but can lead to significant structural damage over time if left untreated. Learning to recognize the common signs of termite activity is crucial for early detection and timely intervention. If you notice any of the following indicators, it's highly recommended to contact a professional termite control service like Greeley Termite for an inspection in Loveland, CO. Ignoring these warning signs can result in costly repairs down the line.

Mud Tubes on Walls or Foundations:

Subterranean termites build mud tubes, often referred to as 'shelter tubes', to travel between their underground colonies and their food source (wood). These tubes are typically the width of a pencil and can be found on exterior or interior walls, foundations, or other surfaces connected to the ground. Discovering these tubes is a strong indicator of active subterranean termite infestation. They provide a protected pathway for termites to move without being exposed to the open air, which can dehydrate them.

Damaged Wood:

Termites consume cellulose, which is a primary component of wood. Infested wood may sound hollow when tapped, appear darker or blistered, or crumble easily. You might also see maze-like patterns within the wood or discover piles of what appears to be sawdust, which is actually termite fecal matter called frass. This frass is often found near termite-damaged wood. The extent of wood damage can vary depending on how long the infestation has been present, but even minor damage can indicate a larger colony at work.

Discarded Termite Wings:

When reproductive termites, known as swarmers, leave their colony to establish new ones, they shed their wings shortly after finding a mate and a suitable location. Finding small, translucent wings scattered on windowsills, near doorways, or in spiderwebs is a common sign of a termite swarm. While swarming events are often brief and may not be noticed, the presence of discarded wings is a clear indication of active termites in or around your property. This often happens in the springtime or after rainfall.

Termite Swarms:

Observing a swarm of flying termites emerging from your property is a definitive sign of a mature termite colony nearby. Termite swarms are often mistaken for flying ants, but termites have straight antennae, uniform waists, and wings of equal size, whereas ants have elbowed antennae, pinched waists, and unequal wing sizes. Swarmers are attracted to light and may be seen near windows and doors. If you witness a termite swarm, it is crucial to contact a pest control professional immediately, as it signifies a significant infestation.

Buckling Paint or Plaster:

Termites working behind walls can cause paint to bubble or buckle and plaster to become discolored or sag. This is due to the moisture termites introduce and the damage they cause to the underlying wood structure. These visual cues often resemble water damage, so it's important to distinguish between the two to ensure proper diagnosis and treatment. Don't assume it's just a leak; investigate further, as it could be termites.

Sagging Floors or Ceilings:

In severe infestations, the structural integrity of wooden floors and ceilings can be compromised by termite damage, leading to noticeable sagging or unevenness. This is a serious sign that requires immediate attention. The internal structure that supports these areas is being eaten away, which can lead to dangerous structural failures if not addressed promptly. This level of damage indicates a long-standing and significant termite problem.

Pinpoint Holes in Drywall:

As termites tunnel through wood behind drywall, they can sometimes chew through the paper layer of the drywall, leaving small, pinpoint-sized holes. These holes may look insignificant but are another potential indicator of hidden termite activity. You might also see tiny piles of frass near these holes. These are often subtle signs that can be easily overlooked if you’re not specifically looking for them.

Subterranean Termites in Wood Piles or Gardens:

Termites are often found in outdoor wood piles, discarded lumber, or even in gardens, especially near the foundation of your home. While finding them in your garden doesn't guarantee an indoor infestation, it significantly increases the risk. Proper storage of firewood away from your home and managing garden debris can help reduce the attractiveness of your property to termites. Termites in these outdoor areas are constantly looking for new food sources, and your home could be next.

Termite Sounds:

While often silent, you might occasionally hear faint clicking or rustling sounds coming from within walls or wooden structures. This could be the sound of termites eating or the sounds of their activity within the wood. Soldier termites, in particular, are known to make a clicking sound by banging their heads against the wood to warn the colony of danger. While not a guaranteed method of detection, being aware of unusual sounds can be helpful.
